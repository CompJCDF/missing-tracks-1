How can we find them?

Each missing persons case is unique. Factors such as risk, age, location, time missing, reason for going missing and so on will determine the urgency of the response and the method.

A case where a toddler has been abducted 24 hours ago in a city centre will be treated very differently to a dementia patient who has been missing in a rural location for some time. All cases are valid and worth looking into. Even in situations where a person has gone missing out of their own choice for a new life or because they feel they’re in danger from people around them, it can be crucial to track those people also to ensure they have adequate protection and that they are not at risk any longer.

The methods for tracking people are incredibly varied and differ according to country and police force. The charity Missing People goes to great lengths to assist the UK police in tracing lost persons. Most people who go missing across the UK of any demographic or situation are found safe and reasonably well but for some they are simply nowhere to be found and the cost incurred with looking for them becomes sadly too great.

New technologies have the ability to transform the plight for tracking missing persons with organisations such as Trace Labs using crowd-sourced open intelligence to do so yet other forms of technology such as facial recognition face great criticism as people fear a totalitarian regime-like state.

This article will look into the various methods and responses used in the hunt for missing people across the UK.

Most missing people are found safe and sound within 72 hours, according to the organization Missing People. These first hours are fundamental to ensure the person’s well-being, but also to protect the integrity of the evidence and avoid that other types of crimes could occur (kidnapping, murdering…).

In the case of adults, investigators try to assess if the person was behaving normally days before going missing. For children, they consider if the child could have been abducted or if he or she could be in imminent danger. While most cases will settle within those first few hours, others can take longer to resolve.

There are no major differences between the duration of the disappearances in the case of children or adults, as the UKMPU report (2016-17) reflects. While around 45 % of adults are found within the first 8 hours, for children this number raises slightly to near 47 %. Another 12 % of adults and 10 % of children will be found within the first week and only a 6 % of grown-ups and 3 % of minors will be found in a period longer than a week.

While some missing people will decide to return home by their own will, others will be found by their carers, the police or family and friends. A 27 % percentage of children in England and Wales will decide to go back home after a few hours or days. For adults, this number goes down to a 19 %. When this is not the case, most children are found by the police (34 %) or their families (18 %). Only a small number are found by their friends (2,5 %). The police is the one that brings more adults back home (more than a 50 %), followed by their families (10 %), while friends account for almost 3 %.

Some missing persons can also travel abroad, either voluntarily or involuntarily, and then it’s when international organizations, such as Interpol, come to play, where people can publish a Yellow Notice for a missing person. These notices are global alerts that get sent to Interpol’s member countries and in some cases, published on their website so that members of the public can see.

The Police Response

Missing Tracks · When someone is reported missing what's the response? - Sam Roberts, North Wales Police

“As soon as we receive the phone call at the control room that the handler will ask a series of questions, I think it's about 25-30 to get the basic information to be able to risk assessment. So we take the basic information, they will then perform their first risk assessment which is to decide who they should go to and how quickly. We use what they call THRIVE (threat, harm, risk, investigation opportunities, vulnerability of the victim and engagement level required.) which decides how quickly you respond to the incident. It will then go through to a supervisor who will agree or disagree with the risk assessment. Depending on what that risk assessment says decides what happens next, say somebody was going to harm themselves, seriously harm themselves, it will then go straight to our force incident manager who then dynamically coordinates that response. And this is an immediate response at this point, we will throw as many available resources at this missing case as possible. That includes obviously helicopters, dogs, whatever we've got at our disposal, all available units in the area will then be sent to that investigation. For all other ones that are lower risk than immediate they go through to our managed response unit, they will do all desk-based enquiries within the first couple of hours, everything is done fairly quickly regardless of the risk level, we deal with it as soon as we get the call. Within a few hours, we begin a desk chase which is hospital calls, phone custody, getting more information to see if this is a risk that can wait a little bit longer rather than an immediate risk.” Sam Roberts, North Wales Police.

Found yet never claimed

The Missing Persons charity provides another service which acts as a heartbreaking gallery of people who have been found dead yet never identified. The profiles of these people are a final plea to anyone who knows them - or once did. Described objectively by their physical features some of the profiles contain photographs of items which were found with the body - shoes, belts, jewellery, small trinkets or religious items. Behind a ‘sensitive content’ agreement statement many include photographs of the bodies themselves.

There is something deeply sad around the stories of people nobody seems to be looking for. The stories behind them tragic, stories it’s likely no one will ever know.

New Ways to Find Missing People

One of the newest and most controversial techniques being considered for use in looking for missing people is facial recognition technology. This biometric software uses CCTV in public places to identify anyone in its range of vision. Just as the latest iPhones can be unlocked with their owner’s face, facial recognition used in public places can identify individuals.

This technology reads nodal points on a human face before using mathematical techniques to translate that data into a unique numerical code or ‘faceprint’. The software then compares that faceprint to a database which holds photos of the public’s faces from open source platforms such as Facebook or from photographs taken at border controls. Once a match is found the software can bring up personal data on an individual such as their name and address.

In England and Wales facial recognition software has been trialled on several occasions. Police facial recognition vans and CCTV cameras have been set up in city centres, shopping districts and football matches. The reasons given for the deployment of the software tend to centre on looking for criminals, finding missing persons and dispelling violent outbreaks. However not everyone feels that the benefits outweigh the potential risks to privacy and the gravitation towards surveillance states.

At the Stratford facial recognition trial in East London, director of Big Brother Watch, Silkie Carlo, protested outside the police van. In an interview with The Guardian, Carlo explained that if facial recognition in public places was not challenged at an early stage we would soon see it rolled out on general CCTV networks. The opposition to the technology is rooted in concerns of privacy which some fear to the kind of invasive authoritarian society which dystopian satires such as 1984 warn of. These projections are not solely based in literature either, in China facial recognition technology has been used by the government to publicly shame people for wearing pyjamas in public, for jaywalking and other so-called ‘uncivilized behaviours’.

When it comes to searching for missing people facial recognition technology could prove to be invaluable in searches for some missing people, providing they have not intentionally gone missing for their own safety. However the risks are that the do-gooding aspects of the software may very well be a Trojan Horse housing a totalitarian police state.

“If it’s a tool to find people, then great, but there are also the ethical issues that come with that”, says Professor Karen Shalev Greene, director of the Centre for the Study of Missing Persons from the University of Portsmouth. While most experts have their concerns about facial recognition, other technologies like GPS trackers and drones are widely supported.

GPS trackers, for example, could be a really convenient tool for people with dementia. These people could wear necklaces or watches so that their families could track their daily activities and ensure their security in case they go missing. Then less families would have to report the cases to the police and actions could be taken earlier on in case they go missing. However, as Shalev Greene explains, this should only be done with the permission of the person involved.

“The issues come when they are so confused that they cannot consent, but then you can also have the discussion within the family between getting their consent or keeping them alive. Personally I think it’s a family decision. I would make that decision for my mother or my grandmother”, the expert explains.

The use of drones is another technique that the Centre for the Study of Missing Persons is looking at. They are way cheaper than a helicopter and can do a great job to access remote areas. They are also not invasive and don’t harm anyone's privacy.

Online communities and apps are also gaining popularity to find missing people. There are around a dozen Facebook groups to find people in the UK. Missing People UK, for example, has more than 16,000 members and Missing Persons South Wales almost 10,000. These online communities in social media are key to resolve some missing cases. Each day, people from all across Britain publish pictures and information about missing persons and help each other with tracks and psychological support.

Apps like Nextdoor, a hyperlocal social media, also help keep neighbourhoods safe and connected. Users of this app submit their real names and addresses and get connected with other users living nearby. Posts are only shared within the same neighbourhood and people can ask all sorts of questions: from restaurant recommendations to missing people.

The Reddit Bureau of Investigation (RBI) is also a popular subreddit within Reddit. With more than 260,000 users, this subreddit “uses the power of the Internet to solve real world problems”. When asked about their opinion, some Redditors are doubtful on how useful it can actually be. “Reddit only ever makes things worse”, a user explains. “RBI is just a forum of sub-forums. Nobody here has any special technology or equipment, it’s just a bunch of regular people trying to make sense of things. In terms of missing people and actual crimes, Reddit as a whole either does nothing or makes things worse. There are some very rare exceptions, obviously”, another user explains.

The rules in the RBI are simple. The first one is that any criminal matters need to be handled by the police. “RBI Is made of a bunch of volunteers who live all over the place. The police are the only ones who can help you with criminal matters. If you have a criminal matter, please contact the police. After the police have fully conducted their investigation or if the investigation has gone cold, you can ask the people of r/RBI”.

They also ask people to share personal information only through private messages and to avoid doxxing, an Internet-based practice of researching and publicly broadcasting or identifying information about an individual or organization.

Even if these groups can have limited results, when the missing cases are no longer investigated by the police, these online platforms are the only way to keep the hopes of families and friends alive.
